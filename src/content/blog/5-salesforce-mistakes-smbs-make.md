Most small and mid-sized businesses don't fail at Salesforce because of the technology. They fail because of decisions made in the first 30 days — before anyone really understood how the team works.

After 11 years of implementations across industries, we keep seeing the same five mistakes. The good news: every single one is fixable.

---

## 1. Buying the wrong edition

Salesforce has multiple editions — Starter, Pro Suite, Enterprise, Unlimited — and the pricing difference between them is significant. Most SMBs either overbuy (paying for features they'll never touch) or underbuy (hitting walls six months in when they need automation or API access).

**The fix:** Map your actual requirements before you buy. If you need Process Builder replacements or complex flows, you need Enterprise at minimum. If you're just managing contacts and pipeline, Pro Suite may be enough for now.

---

## 2. Going live without cleaning your data

Importing dirty data into Salesforce doesn't clean it — it just makes your CRM dirty too. Duplicate contacts, inconsistent naming, missing fields — they all follow you in.

**The fix:** Do a data audit before migration. Deduplicate, standardise naming conventions, and agree on what "required" actually means for your team. It's unglamorous work, but it saves months of pain later.

---

## 3. Over-customising too early

Salesforce is incredibly flexible, which is exactly the problem. New customers often build out complex custom objects, fields, and page layouts in week one — before they know what they actually need.

**The fix:** Start with standard objects (Leads, Contacts, Accounts, Opportunities). Use them as-is for 60–90 days. You'll learn more about how your team actually works from real usage than from any planning session.

---

## 4. Skipping user training

This one is common and painful. A perfectly configured Salesforce org that nobody knows how to use is worthless. If your team doesn't log in, your data stays stale and leadership loses trust in the system fast.

**The fix:** Budget time for training as part of the implementation — not as an afterthought. Short, role-based sessions work better than a single all-hands walkthrough. And appoint an internal champion who can answer day-to-day questions.

---

## 5. Treating the go-live as the finish line

Salesforce implementations are not a one-and-done project. Processes change, teams grow, and the platform gets updated three times a year. Businesses that treat the go-live as the end point end up with an org that slowly drifts out of alignment with how the business actually works.

**The fix:** Plan for a 90-day post-launch review at minimum. Look at what's being used, what's being ignored, and what new pain points have surfaced. Small, regular adjustments beat a major overhaul every two years.

---

## The common thread

Every mistake above comes down to the same thing: moving too fast without enough context. The best Salesforce implementations we've been part of spent more time on discovery than on configuration.
